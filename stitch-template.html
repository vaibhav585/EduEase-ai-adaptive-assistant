The Observer Effect in Quantum Mechanics

In quantum mechanics, the observer effect is the disturbance of an observed system by the act of observation. This is often confused with the uncertainty principle, though they are distinct concepts.

While the uncertainty principle describes a fundamental limit to how accurately certain pairs of physical properties can be known, the observer effect relates specifically to the physical interaction required to measure a system.

"If you want to find the secrets of the universe, think in terms of energy, frequency and vibration." — Nikola Tesla

Imagine a particle like an electron. To "see" it, we must bounce a photon off it. However, the photon carries energy and momentum, which will change the electron's path and speed the moment they interact. Thus, the very act of looking changes the outcome.

Mathematical Foundations

The wave function, denoted as Ψ (psi), represents the probability distribution of a particle's properties. When an measurement occurs, this wave function is said to "collapse" into a single definite state.
